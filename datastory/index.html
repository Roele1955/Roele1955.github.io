How coupons impact sales?

Our dataset contains information about shopping history for a subset of our customers. We will try to analyze how coupons are used by families and how they change shopping habits.
Coupons are usually released as part of some campaign aimed to promote certain products. The advertising has a cost from the point of view of the retailer, therefore it must result in some kind of advantage in sales for the store to be beneficial.

Measuring the effect of a campaign is a complex problem, many hambiental factor coinfluence customers and it is almost impossible to have an exact impact measure. Therefore, we will restrict our analysis to a particular effect: we are mostly interested in how a coupon redemption impact the shopping habits of the customer.
It often happen we buy a product just out of curiosity because there is a discount, we are naturally attracted by a deal that seems advantageous. Then if we like what we bought we start buying it, then this initial discount resulted in a gain for the retailer that compensate the loss of profit in the first shop.
More in general, we want to highlight the difference of purchase before and after a coupon redemption.

The transaction data holds precious information about coupons, but it is not directly available. The first step was identifying the exact transactions in which coupons were used. For that, we crossed the coupon redemption table with some of the attributes in the transaction table, such as:

  • the household that made the transaction
  • the day of the transaction
  • the product bought
  • whether any discount was applied

Once the coupon transactions were identified, it was time to estimate the success of those coupon transactions. We decided to use a statistic that didn't only take into account the sales during the campaign, but also any long-lasting effect on the consumption of the products redeemed by the household.

So, we computed the "estimated total sales increase" as the difference between the daily average sales before and after the coupon was used, multiplied by the number of days from the day the coupon was used until the end of the survey.

This statistic was computed for every coupon transaction, so the daily averages were only computed for the product and the household in that particular coupon transaction.

Campaign success

Campaign success is derived as the sum of the "estimated sales increase" of every coupon transaction associated to the campaign.

Three campaigns stand out: 8, 13 and 18.

When look at the number of coupons redeemed over time, these three camapigns are clearly observable.

The following analysis tries to shed some light on why these three particular campaigns were so successful.

The duration of these campaigns is not related to their success. As a matter of fact, campaign 15 had the longest campaign duartion by far, but had an estimated sales increase close to zero.

One might think that these campaigns had a higher number of coupons than others. As we see they did, but also did campaigns 26 and 30 and they were not nearly as successful.

However, when we look at the number of families that received these campaigns and at the number of families that participated in them (by at least redeeming one coupon), they really stand out.

Such a big gap might be explained by the fact that these campaigns were targeted at a national level, wheareas others were targeted locally.

Since the demographic data available doesn't contain any indication on where the households live, the best way to indicate how spread a campaign was might be to count the number of different stores in which the coupons associated to that campaign were redeemed. And as we can see, campaigns 8, 13 and 18 were in fact targeted on a larger scale than others.

Customer's demographic

First, let’s introduce our customers: frequent buyers .
People who often go shopping to our chain of supermarkets and decided to register in our database to obtain some benefits. Those advantages are usually quite diverse and can go from a special discount to loyalty points.

Data about shopping history for these people has been collected over 2 years : what they bought, when and in which supermarket.
If we take a look at the collected information we can observe that not all the registered families have a complete history, for around half of them we have no information for the first 10 weeks of our survey.
This may be due to many reasons: after the survey started, people would need to give the consent to collect their informations and this can create a buffer of time in which people are joining before we register data in fulls wing.
The image below show the amount of families active in our survey over the 2 years.

We also notice that some families have not more information after a certain day, probably they stopped to buy in our chain of supermarkets or they decided to not participate anymore in the survey.
Note that the final decrease is due to the end of the survey: not all families shop daily, therefore, most of them have not bought anything in the last few days of the survey.

Furthermore, some of our customers (around 800) provided their demographic information:

  • yearly family income : nit income of their family over one year, expressed in USA dollars
  • age range
  • family composition: if they live alone or with husband/wife, if they have kids
  • house information: owned or rented

This demographic data is clearly not indicative of the whole population, but we try to see which households are more popular of the ones which provide their data.

Products

We will now analyze which products are sold in our supermarkets.

Every shop has a general common structure: each shop groups its products in different departments that sell a particular group of items. This division is quite logical: assume you are shopping and you want to buy some fruits, it would be really difficult to look for apples all over a shop.
From the department of our supermarkets we can get a general idea of which kind of shop were interested by the survey.

We see that 'GROCERY' and 'DRUG GM' are the department that contains the largest variety of products.
Below you can see which products have greater choice for those departments.

Coupons

Now that we know which kind of products and departments are most popular, let us move on to the coupons. Coupons are sent to customers as parts of different campaigns. Each coupon is redeemable for some subset of products. Let us now see the number of coupons issued for products grouped department-wise.

So, we see that larger number of coupons are issued for the more popular departments, which is what we would expect, if the stores wish the coupons to be redeemed more.
